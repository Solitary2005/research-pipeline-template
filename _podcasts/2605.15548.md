## Paper

**[KaRMA: A Kinematic Metric for Fine Manipulation Ability in Robotic Hands](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.15548)** — Martin Peticco et.al.

## Summary Card

### Motivation
Traditional robotic hand metrics like workspace volume or grasp stability do not directly measure dexterity—the ability to continuously change an object's pose within the hand while maintaining contact. This gap hinders objective comparison and design iteration for fine manipulation tasks, such as precision assembly or in-hand reorientation, where regrasping is undesirable.

### Method
KaRMA (Kinematic Rolling Manipulation Ability) is a kinematics-only metric that evaluates a two-finger precision pinch on a spherical test object under rolling contact. It uses breadth-first search over translation and rotation primitives to explore reachable in-hand object poses, enforcing joint limits, collision constraints, rolling contact, and antipodal force feasibility. The metric outputs three scores: translational coverage (KaRMA-T), rotational coverage (KaRMA-R), and sensitivity to the initial grasp (KaRMA-S).

### Key Results
Evaluated on 16 robotic hands, KaRMA separates hands that rank identically under static proxies (e.g., DOF count), reveals translation–rotation tradeoffs invisible to existing baselines, and is qualitatively consistent with published task benchmarks. It provides a standardized, model-based score that avoids the system-level dependencies of full task benchmarks.

### Takeaways
KaRMA offers an objective, kinematic-only tool for hand procurement and design iteration, complementing static proxies and task benchmarks. Its focus on rolling contact in a precision pinch captures a fundamental aspect of dexterity, enabling more informed comparisons across diverse hand designs and highlighting tradeoffs that static metrics miss.
