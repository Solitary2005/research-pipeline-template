## Paper

**[Hand-in-the-Loop: Improving Dexterous VLA via Seamless Interventional Correction](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.15157)** — Zhuohang Li, Liqun Huang, Wei Xu et al.

## Summary Card

### Motivation
Vision-Language-Action (VLA) models suffer from compounding errors in dexterous manipulation due to high-dimensional action spaces and contact-rich dynamics. Interactive Imitation Learning (IIL) can refine policies via human takeover, but direct teleoperation takeover causes abrupt 'gesture jumps'—command discontinuities that destabilize grasps and cause object drops. This work addresses the critical need for seamless human-in-the-loop correction in high-degree-of-freedom bimanual dexterous tasks.

### Method
Hand-in-the-Loop (HandITL) introduces an optimization-based relative hand retargeting that maps incremental fingertip motion instead of absolute pose, and a velocity-based shared control for arms that injects transient residual twists atop policy commands. This fusion avoids gesture jumps at takeover moments, enabling smooth intervention. Two modes are supported: full takeover for critical recovery and copilot shared control for small corrections.

### Key Results
HandITL reduces takeover jitter by 99.8% compared to direct teleoperation, decreases grasp failures by 87.5%, and cuts mean completion time by 19.1%. When used to collect on-policy intervention data, fine-tuned policies outperform those trained with standard teleoperation data by 19% on average across three long-horizon dexterous tasks involving bimanual coordination, tool use, and fine-grained manipulation.

### Takeaways
This work demonstrates that seamless human-in-the-loop correction is both feasible and critical for scaling VLA policies to dexterous manipulation. The proposed relative retargeting and shared-control paradigm effectively bridges the command mismatch between human teleoperation and policy execution, enabling practical IIL for high-DoF hands. These results highlight the importance of intervention data quality and smooth authority transitions for robust policy improvement in contact-rich robotic tasks.
