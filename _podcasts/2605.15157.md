## Paper

**[Hand-in-the-Loop: Improving Dexterous VLA via Seamless Interventional Correction](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.15157)** — Zhuohang Li, Liqun Huang, Wei Xu et al.

## Summary Card

### Motivation
Vision-Language-Action (VLA) models for dexterous manipulation suffer from compounding errors in high-dimensional action spaces and contact-rich dynamics, leading to out-of-distribution states during long-horizon tasks. Interactive Imitation Learning (IIL) can mitigate this via human intervention, but applying it to high-DoF hands is hindered by 'gesture jumps'—large command discontinuities when switching from policy execution to human teleoperation, which destabilize grasps and cause failures.

### Method
HandITL introduces a seamless human-in-the-loop intervention method that blends human corrective intent with autonomous policy execution to avoid gesture jumps. It uses an optimization-based relative hand retargeting that maps incremental fingertip motion (not absolute pose) to the robot hand, along with a velocity-based arm shared control that injects residual twists into policy-predicted commands. This enables smooth takeover and supports both full takeover and copilot shared control modes for bimanual tasks.

### Key Results
HandITL reduces takeover jitter by 99.8% compared to direct teleoperation takeover, decreases grasp failures by 87.5%, and reduces mean completion time by 19.1% in real-world bimanual tasks. Policies fine-tuned with HandITL-collected intervention data outperform those trained with standard teleoperation data by 19% on average across three long-horizon dexterous tasks.

### Takeaways
HandITL demonstrates that seamless intervention is critical for applying IIL to dexterous manipulation, enabling effective on-policy correction data collection without destabilizing contact-rich interactions. The approach highlights the importance of relative motion retargeting and shared control for high-DoF systems, and suggests that targeted interventional data can substantially improve VLA policy robustness and long-horizon performance in complex manipulation scenarios.
